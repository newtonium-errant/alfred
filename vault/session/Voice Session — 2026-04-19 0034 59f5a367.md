**Alfred** (01:02): That's a clean example. Let me break down what's actually doing the work:

**Headline mechanics**

- "My Girlfriend Has The Worst Taste In Men" — full bro-influencer cadence: possessive framing, complaint-about-partner, clickbait promise of drama/vindication
- "In Black Movies" — the swerve. Three words retroactively rewrite the whole sentence. "Taste in men" → "taste in [Men in Black] movies." Grammatically it was always a film-franchise take.
- The pivot is **lexical**, not tonal — same sentence, different parse tree. Reader's brain commits to parse A, gets forced into parse B.

**Subtitle mechanics**

- "But this essay is more about..." — explicit meta-move. Names the bait, names the switch.
- "...learning to show up for my partner in the little ways AND the big ways" — earnest, the genuine subject
- "because it all matters" — sincere closer, no irony hedge
- The ALL CAPS "AND" is a small bro-influencer residue — one stylistic tell that the persona hasn't fully dropped, which keeps it playful instead of preachy

**Template structure I'd extract**

1. **Headline**: construct a sentence with a forced garden-path parse. The first N words read as bro-influencer bait (relationship complaint, hot take, flex, grievance). The last 1–3 words reframe the whole thing into something innocuous or nerdy.
   - Mechanism options: lexical ambiguity (Men/Men in Black), category swap (my ex/my ex-landlord), scope collapse (the worst year of my life/the worst year of my fantasy football league)
2. **Subtitle**: "But this essay is actually about [earnest thing], because [sincere reason]." Signal the rug-pull explicitly. The earnest payload should be genuinely the point — not a joke about being earnest.
3. **Residue**: keep one stylistic tic from the bro voice in the subtitle (ALL CAPS word, "literally," em-dash flex) so it reads as a persona you're wearing, not one you're mocking from outside.

**Variations worth trying**

- Pure lexical swerve (your MIB example)
- Proper-noun swerve: "How I Finally Dumped My Toxic Ex / Parrot, Steve, who I rehomed to a sanctuary"
- Scale swerve: "The Betrayal That Broke Me / A sourdough starter named Gary"
- Scope swerve: "Why I'll Never Forgive Her / my GPS, for routing me through Moncton"

**Failure modes to watch**

- If the earnest subject is too small, the swerve reads as dismissive of real essays about partnership/grief/etc. MIB works because the real subject (showing up for your partner) is *more* serious than the fake one.
- If the bait is too mean-spirited, the persona shift lands as "gotcha" not "playful." Keep the bait targeting *the narrator's own persona*, not a real person.
- Meta-fatigue: this trick doesn't scale to every essay. It's a format, not a voice.
